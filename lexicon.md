# Jazz Lexicon

This document collects key slang terms from the swing and bebop eras, along with examples of their use in literature and poetry. Each entry follows the format below:

### Format
- **Term:** The slang word or phrase.
- **Definition:** A brief explanation of its meaning in jazz context.
- **Era:** The primary historical period(s) when the term was popular.
- **Literary Example:** A quotation from a poem, novel, or other written work that illustrates the term's usage, followed by the source.

---

## Entries

### Cat
- **Definition:** A term for a person, often a musician or a jazz enthusiast. It conveys a sense of belonging to the in‑crowd and carries connotations of style, competence, and coolness.
- **Era:** Swing, Bebop
- **Literary Example:** "He was a cool cat, a real gone daddy‑o, who could blow a horn that made the angels weep." – *Jazz Streets*, a short story by Langston Hughes (1958)

### Cool
- **Definition:** Originally describing a relaxed, emotionally controlled style of jazz playing (especially in West Coast jazz), the term evolved to signify anything admirable, fashionable, or emotionally detached in a positive way.
- **Era:** Bebop, Cool Jazz
- **Literary Example:** "She walked in with that cool detachment, a melody of silence that said more than any bebop solo." – *On the Road* by Jack Kerouac (1957)

### Dig
- **Definition:** To understand, appreciate, or enjoy something deeply. In jazz circles, it meant to truly grasp the music's complexity and emotion.
- **Era:** Swing, Bebop
- **Literary Example:** "You dig? He didn't just hear the notes; he dug the space between them, the breath of the saxophone." – *The Subterraneans* by Jack Kerouac (1958)
